Albania, a small country located on the Balkan Peninsula, is often overlooked by travelers seeking adventures and beauty in southeastern Europe. However, this country, known for its rich history, stunning nature, and hospitable people, offers visitors many reasons to explore it. If you have never been to Albania and are looking for useful information, here are some key points you should know.

## Culture and History 🌄

Albania has a rich and diverse history, ranging from ancient Illyrians, through the periods of the Roman and Ottoman Empires, to modern times. The country is home to many historical landmarks, including ancient cities like Butrint and Gjirokastër, which are UNESCO World Heritage sites. Albanians are proud of their cultural identity and traditions, which you can experience everywhere - from traditional music and dance, to local cuisine and the hospitality they offer.

For most of the second half of the 20th century, Albania was sealed off from the rest of the world under one of Europe's strictest communist regimes, which is part of why it stayed off the typical traveler's map for so long. That isolation left behind a strange but fascinating layer of history - from bunkers scattered across the countryside to Cold War-era museums in the capital, Tirana - alongside a much older Mediterranean and Ottoman heritage that goes back thousands of years.

## Nature and Beaches 🌊

One of Albania's biggest attractions is its natural beauty. The country offers stunning mountains, lakes, and rivers, ideal for hiking, cycling, and other outdoor activities. The Albanian coastline along the Adriatic and Ionian seas is dotted with beautiful beaches that remain relatively undiscovered and are not crowded with tourists. Beaches like Ksamil, Dhermi, and Himara are known for their clear waters and picturesque scenery.

The stretch of coast south of Vlorë, known as the Albanian Riviera, is where the mountains drop almost straight into the sea, and it's where most of the postcard-worthy beaches are found:

- **Ksamil:** Tiny islands you can wade or swim to, turquoise water, and a laid-back vibe - often compared to the Caribbean.
- **Dhermi:** A longer beach backed by mountains, with a livelier strip of beach bars in the summer season.
- **Himara:** A good base for exploring the Riviera, with a mix of quieter coves and a proper town with restaurants and shops.
- **Gjipe Beach:** Reachable only by a short hike or boat, tucked into a dramatic canyon - one of the most photogenic spots on the whole coast.

For nature beyond the coast, the Albanian Alps in the north (around Theth and Valbona) offer some of the best - and least crowded - hiking in the Balkans, with a hiking trail connecting the two valleys that's become a favorite among backpackers.

## Practical Information 💡

- **Visas and entry:** EU citizens do not need a visa for stays up to 90 days.
- **Currency:** Albanian Lek (ALL). In tourist areas, euros are often accepted.
- **Language:** The official language is Albanian, but in tourist areas, you can often communicate in English or Italian.
- **Transport:** Albania has a growing network of buses and shared taxis (furgons). Renting a car is also a common and convenient option for exploring the country.
- **Best time to visit:** Late spring (May-June) and early autumn (September) offer warm weather without the peak-summer crowds and price spikes along the Riviera.

## Driving in Albania 🚗

If you plan to drive in Albania, it is recommended to have an International Driving Permit (IDP) in addition to your national driving license. The IDP is recognized in Albania and can be useful in case of any encounters with local authorities or car rental agencies.

The coastal road along the Riviera (the SH8) is stunning but slow and winding, with sharp drop-offs in places - budget more time than the map suggests, and take the hairpin turns carefully, especially after dark. Mountain roads in the north can be unpaved, so a vehicle with higher clearance is worth considering if you're heading toward Theth or Valbona.

## Tips for Travelers ✈️

- **Explore off-season:** The main tourist season is in the summer, when the beaches are most visited. For a quieter vacation, consider visiting in spring or fall.
- **Try local cuisine:** Albanian cuisine is influenced by Mediterranean and Balkan traditions. Try traditional dishes like byrek, tave kosi, or fresh seafood.
- **Respect local customs:** Albanians are known for their hospitality and respect for traditions. Being polite and respecting local customs will ensure you have a pleasant experience.
- **Carry some cash:** Card payments are increasingly common in cities and resorts, but smaller guesthouses, tavernas, and furgons along the coast often expect cash.

## Frequently asked questions about Albania

**Is Albania safe for tourists?**
Yes - Albania is generally considered safe for travelers, with normal city-travel precautions around belongings in busy areas. The biggest practical risks are usually related to driving on narrow mountain or coastal roads rather than crime.

**How many days do you need in Albania?**
A week gives you a solid taste: a couple of days in Tirana and the historic towns inland, then four or five days along the Riviera for beaches and day trips like Gjipe or Ksamil.

**Is Albania cheap to visit?**
Compared to neighboring Greece or Italy, yes - accommodation, food, and transport are noticeably cheaper, especially outside the peak summer weeks on the Riviera.
