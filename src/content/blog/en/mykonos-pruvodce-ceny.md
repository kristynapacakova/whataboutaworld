"Welcome to the island of wind" - that's exactly how we were greeted on Mykonos. And I can confirm they weren't lying. The wind followed us everywhere, but at those temperatures it's more of a blessing than a hassle - the locals call it the meltemi, and it pleasantly tousles your hair on the beach and cools your skin after a day of sunbathing. Mykonos is an island where luxury yachts meet cracked sidewalks, where breakfast costs as much as dinner back home, yet you can still find a quiet, crowd-free beach if you know where to look. That's exactly what this guide is about. If you're planning a trip, here's my honest rundown based on my own experience in July - no rose-tinted glasses, just love for this slightly chaotic island.

## A bit of history before you go

Mykonos is part of the Cyclades, an island group in the middle of the Aegean Sea, named after the mythical hero Mykonos, grandson of the sun god Apollo. For centuries it was a poor, fairly unremarkable fishing island - the turning point came in the 1960s, when it became a favorite of the international jet set and, later, celebrities. Today it's one of the priciest and most glamorous islands in the Mediterranean, but the old fishing-village soul is still there underneath it all, especially outside peak season.

## When to go? July surprised me

If you're looking for the ideal balance, I'd recommend July specifically. There weren't as many tourists as I expected, the weather was reliably sunny, and the temperatures were perfect for tanning (unless you're like me and forget the sunscreen). For reference, here's how the island behaves through the year:

- **May and June:** Warm, but the sea is still chilly, fewer tourists, lower accommodation prices.
- **July and August:** Peak season, highest prices, the most boats in the harbor, but also the best atmosphere in the clubs and bars.
- **September:** My secret recommendation - the sea is at its warmest all year, the crowds are already thinning out, and prices start dropping.
- **October onward:** Plenty of places close down, and the island returns to its quieter, local pace.

## Flights and accommodation: where you can actually save

Traveling to Mykonos doesn't have to wreck your budget if you plan ahead:

- **Flights:** We booked a month in advance. A round-trip Ryanair ticket from Vienna cost us a great 50 EUR. To save even more, keep an eye on direct flights from Athens - they're often cheaper than international connections.
- **Where we stayed:** Finding accommodation was a bit of a challenge since prices climbed every day. We ended up choosing the White and Grey Hotel - a great location (a few minutes from both the airport and the center), a beautiful pool, and a heavenly served breakfast that changed every day.
- **The luxury alternative:** If budget isn't a concern, one of the island's most iconic luxury hotels is the legendary Cavo Tagoo.
- **An important safety note:** It's not talked about much, but crime on Mykonos has risen since covid alongside the surge in tourism. That's why I'd recommend not staying right in the busy town center, but a little further out (like White and Grey), where it's quieter and safer.

## Mykonos Town: a walk among windmills and Little Venice

The island's capital, known locally as Chora, is a maze of whitewashed alleys where getting lost is basically the plan. It's not an accident - the streets were originally built narrow and winding on purpose, to confuse pirates. It works on tourists too, so don't be afraid to wander - you'll stumble onto something beautiful anyway.

- **The Windmills (Kato Mili):** Mykonos's iconic postcard view. They're at their best right before and after sunset, when the golden light hits them.
- **Little Venice:** Houses built right above the sea, balconies almost splashed by the waves. A great spot for a sunset drink, even if you pay a little extra for the view.
- **Matoyianni Street:** The main shopping street, full of boutiques, jewelers, and sweet shops - perfect for an evening stroll, even if you're only window-shopping.
- **Little Venice before dusk:** If you want a photo without a crowd in the frame, come a couple of hours earlier, before the main sunset wave arrives.

## A day trip to Delos: a slice of history just around the corner

If you have more than three days on the island, take a half-day trip to Delos - the sacred island of ancient Greece, reachable by boat from the port in Mykonos Town in just a few dozen minutes. It's an archaeological site with not a single modern building, so it feels almost surreal after a few days surrounded by bars and pools. According to legend, Apollo and Artemis were born here, and for thousands of years the island was a religious center for the entire Aegean. Bring a hat and plenty of water - shade is basically nonexistent there.

## Getting around the island

Mykonos is smaller than people think, but buses don't run exactly on schedule and taxis are hard to find in the evening. What worked best for us:

- **Renting a car or ATV:** Gives you the most freedom, especially if you want to reach more remote beaches like Agios Sostis or Fokos.
- **Local buses:** A cheap option between Mykonos Town and the main beaches, though they get packed in peak season.
- **Walking and hitchhiking:** Sounds crazy, but locals treat it as completely normal for shorter distances.

## What to see: my favorite spots

On Mykonos, you can't get away without a reservation at the better venues. I'd recommend booking ahead at places like Zuma Restaurant, 180 Sunset Bar, or Scorpio.

### 1. Super Paradise Beach: party, luxury, and snorkeling

This is a place you have to experience. But if you want to save your nerves and your money, stick to my tips:

- **Parking:** Head to the first lot on the left. The staff takes your keys, parks the car for you, and it's free.
- **Sunbeds:** Prices are tiered by row (1st row 150 €, 2nd row 100 €, 3rd row 80 € per person). Right next to it, though, there's a public stretch of beach where you can lay your own towel.
- **Food and service:** A drink costs around 23 €, and the club sandwiches are huge and filling. In total, we paid 206 EUR for sunbeds and food for two.
- **Watch the tip:** The waiter asked us for a 10% tip (not mandatory!). Given the already steep service prices, don't be afraid to politely decline.

### 2. 180 Sunset Bar: sunset with goosebumps

This bar requires a so-called "minimum spend," which in our stage-side zone was 70 EUR per person.

- **The experience:** Book a spot by the stage. Live music, an incredible singer, and dancers during sunset - it's an experience that beats any party on the island. The show wraps up around 10:30 pm.
- **Getting there:** Parking at the bar costs 15 EUR. Greece has a 0.5‰ blood alcohol limit, but I'd suggest an Uber or walking instead.

### 3. Quieter beaches, if you need a break from the crowd

Not every day has to be a party day. For a slower pace, try Agios Sostis (no sunbeds, just nature and a couple of tavernas) or Fokos Beach in the north of the island - the road getting there is rough, but the reward is an almost empty beach even in July.

## Food you have to try

- **Kopanisti** - a spicy local cheese you'll find as a side dish almost everywhere.
- **Louza** - a cured pork specialty typical of Mykonos specifically.
- **Fresh fish and charcoal-grilled octopus** - best at the small tavernas away from the main waterfront promenade, where both the price and the quality beat the big seaside restaurants.
- **Local Cycladic wine** - light, slightly mineral, and a great match for seafood.

## What to pack

- **A snorkel** - the sea by Super Paradise Beach is incredibly clear, and there are water sports right next to the beach too.
- **A wind-proof hat or at least a hair tie** - the meltemi is no joke.
- **High-SPF sunscreen** - the sun in the Cyclades is more intense than you'd expect, even in a "milder" July.
- **Cash for small expenses** - not every beach spot takes cards, especially the smaller snack stands.
- **Comfortable sandals for uneven streets** - the cobblestones in Chora are beautiful but not exactly ankle-friendly.

## Frequently asked questions about Mykonos

**Is Mykonos good for families with kids?**
Yes, but choose your beach and area carefully - club beaches like Super Paradise are aimed at a younger, louder crowd, while spots like Ornos or Platis Gialos are calmer and more family-friendly.

**How many days do you need on Mykonos?**
Three to four days cover the highlights - the beaches, Chora, and Little Venice. If you also want the Delos day trip and a few slower days, plan for a week.

**Is Mykonos expensive outside of peak season?**
Significantly cheaper. In May, June, or September, accommodation and restaurant prices drop by tens of percent compared to the peak of summer.

## Final tip

Mykonos is an island of extremes - luxury right next to the simple life of locals, expensive clubs right next to free public stretches of beach. The secret to a happy trip is mixing both: splurge on a front-row sunbed one day, then bring your own towel and snorkel the next and go find a quieter bay. That's how you get the most out of Mykonos without feeling like you blew your whole budget on a single weekend.
